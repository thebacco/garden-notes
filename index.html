Definitions

Fruit-Fill Crops
A practical feeding group for crops already carrying harvestable fruit. The goal is not more foliage; it is carbohydrate movement, potassium-supported fruit quality, steady water, and calcium transport in tomato/eggplant fruit.
Solanaceous Fruit
Fruit from Solanaceae crops such as tomato and eggplant. These crops can show blossom-end rot when calcium movement into rapidly expanding fruit is disrupted, often by irregular water uptake rather than absent soil calcium.
Cucurbits
Members of Cucurbitaceae, including zucchini and squash. Summer squash are commonly monoecious: separate male and female flowers on the same plant. Fruit set depends on female flower production, pollen transfer, water status, and harvest pressure.
Greens & Stalks
Vegetative crops where the eaten part is leaf, petiole, or grass-family tissue. Nitrogen and water drive usable growth, but calcium transport and even moisture still matter, especially for celery blackheart.
Roots & Storage Organs
Crops grown for underground tissues. They usually benefit from steady baseline fertility rather than repeated liquid pushes, because excessive nitrogen and moisture swings can reduce storage quality.

How Much To Apply

Core rule Mix ratio is concentration, not plant dose

A 1-gallon jug of liquid feed gets divided across plants. Do not assume the whole gallon goes into one plant unless the soil volume and moisture actually need that much.

Dry granular Tomato-tone is not mixed as a jug

Sprinkle around the outer root zone, keep it off stems, scratch into the top inch, then water in. For established containers, use about 1.5 tsp per 4 inches of pot diameter; for single large plants, cap normal top-dress at about 3 Tbsp per plant.

0.5 cu ft container About a 3.7-gallon soil volume

Liquid feed: start with 2 cups per plant, use up to 4 cups if the container is dry or heavily rooted, and stop when the mix begins to drain. Tomato-tone: usually 1 to 1.5 Tbsp around the edge; use 3 Tbsp only for a big hungry tomato/squash-style plant.

6 cu ft bed About a 45-gallon soil volume

Liquid feed: spread 1 gallon across roughly 6 to 10 sq ft, or split it among 3 to 5 medium plants / 2 to 3 large hungry plants. Tomato-tone: dose by plant count, not bed volume: about 3 Tbsp per tomato, eggplant, zucchini, or squash; lighter crops get about half.

Liquid products Pour at the root zone

Use plain water first if soil is bone dry. Then apply the mixed fertilizer around the drip line/root zone, not against the stem. For strawberries/herbs, use a lighter pour: about 0.5 to 1 cup per plant.

If Tiger Bloom hit tomato/eggplant Count it as this cycle's bloom feed

It is okay during flowering/fruiting. Log it, plain-water next, and skip Farmer's Secret for 7-10 days so you are not stacking bloom concentrates. Resume Tomato Booster only if plants are still actively flowering/fruiting and leaves are not showing burn.

Tiger Bloom vs Tomato Booster Both bloom feeds, different jobs

Tiger Bloom is a general 2-8-4 bloom push, useful when you want phosphorus-heavy flower support. Tomato Booster is the preferred tomato/eggplant finisher: 3-8-7, more potassium for fruit fill, and tomato-specific calcium/iron/humic support.

Safety check Water response beats the calendar

If water runs straight through, slow down and split the dose. If soil stays soggy, skip liquid feed. If leaves look burned or overly lush, back off the next feeding.

Uses

Base Compost and worm castings

Use before planting or as a light side-dress to support soil structure.

Growth Fish emulsion or blood meal

Nitrogen-forward feeds for leafy growth. Go easy once fruiting starts.

Fruit Tomato/vegetable fertilizer

Balanced or bloom-leaning blends for tomatoes, peppers, and cucumbers.

Stress Kelp meal or liquid kelp

Useful around transplanting, heat stress, and recovery weeks.

Decision Triggers

If leaves need pushing Use Alaska Fish

Celery, kale, or corn look pale, slow, dry-stressed, or hungry for foliage.

If bloom support is needed Use Tiger Bloom

Zucchini/squash are the main target. Tomatoes or eggplant can use it as the bloom feed for that cycle; skip Farmer's Secret that week.

If fruit needs finishing Use Farmer's Secret

Tomatoes, eggplant, or strawberries are in late-season fruit fill and ripening mode.

If base feed is due Use Tomato-tone

Containers need the mid-season slow-release top-dress and calcium support.

If roots are fed Do nothing

Leeks and horseradish get one light Tomato-tone feed, then leave them alone.
